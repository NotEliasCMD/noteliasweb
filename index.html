Agentic AI · Reliability

            
Agentic triage for Facebook's network

            
    
              
  • Spark
  • XGBoost
  • LangGraph
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
Facebook's network runs on live hardware emitting petabytes of telemetry a day, and when a link or box quietly degrades, latency spikes and services drop. Handling it ticket by ticket couldn't keep pace with growth — engineers drowned in false positives while the failures that mattered slipped through.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I started with petabyte-scale exploratory analysis of years of tickets and failure logs to find where the system actually bottlenecked. From there I trained tree-based models to predict machine health and triage incoming tickets, then wrapped them in agentic workflows that choose and carry out a fix — every action gated by real-engineer validation.

          

          

            
What I built

            
An end-to-end triage loop — predict, decide, act, verify — that resolves issues before they surface as outages.

            
    
              
  • Petabyte-scale EDA pipeline surfacing real failure modes
    • 
              
  • Tree-based models scoring machine health and triaging tickets
    • 
              
  • Human-validated agentic workflows that reroute and self-mitigate
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
Monthly ticket volume dropped 40% as the system auto-resolved false positives and self-healed before impact, and the network held 98% uptime while it kept growing. Engineers were freed from repetitive triage to focus on the failures that genuinely needed them.

What I built

            
A per-region CPM pricing system — ingest, clean, validate, predict — that plugs directly into the ad-sales platform.

            
    
              
  • Ingestion cleaning tens of dirty impression feeds

    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
The first integration delivered $2M immediately through smarter upsell pricing. Sales teams finally had per-region CPM estimates they could quote with confidence, instead of one blunt national number that fit no market well.

Insurance · Pricing

            
US auto insurance pricing model

            
    
              
  • Python
  • XGBoost
  • GLM
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
Pricing US auto insurance means setting a premium that covers expected losses without pricing yourself out of a fiercely competitive market. Rate the risk too high and you lose the customer; too low and the book bleeds on claims.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I engineered features from driver history, vehicle characteristics and geography, then modeled expected claim frequency and severity. I blended a transparent GLM baseline the actuaries could reason about with gradient-boosted models that captured the non-linear interactions, calibrated against years of historical loss experience.

          

          

            
What I built

            
A production pricing model that scores expected loss cost per policy and feeds the rating engine.

            
    
              
  • Frequency and severity models on historical losses
    • 
              
  • GLM baseline paired with gradient-boosted lift
    • 
              
  • Calibration keeping premiums competitive and adequate
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
Pricing moved from flat rating tables to per-policy risk cost, tightening premiums to actual risk so the book could grow without underpricing it. Actuaries kept an interpretable baseline they could defend to regulators, with the ML layer adding accuracy where it mattered.

Specialty · Tail Risk

            
Detecting black swans in specialty lines

            
    
              
  • Python
  • SciPy
  • XGBoost
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
Specialty lines are dominated by rare, catastrophic losses — the black swans that barely appear in the data but decide whether the book is profitable. Models trained to fit the common case are blind to exactly the tail that matters most.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I focused on the tail rather than the mean: extreme-value analysis of historical loss distributions, plus features that flag the early conditions preceding large losses. I paired that with gradient-boosted classifiers tuned for the severe-but-rare class instead of overall accuracy.

          

          

            
What I built

            
A tail-risk detection layer that scores specialty exposures for black-swan potential before binding.

            
    
              
  • Extreme-value modeling of historical loss tails
    • 
              
  • Features surfacing early signals of severe losses
    • 
              
  • Classifiers tuned for the rare severe class
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
Underwriters gained an early-warning score on the exposures most likely to blow up the book, shifting attention from routine risks to the handful of accounts carrying genuine tail danger — where a single missed black swan outweighs hundreds of ordinary policies.

Computer Vision · Claims

            
Computer vision for write-off valuation

            
    
              
  • YOLO
  • PyTorch
  • OpenCV
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
When a car is written off, the insurer owns it and needs to value the salvage accurately — but damage assessment was manual, slow and inconsistent. Getting the write-off decision or the residual value wrong costs money on every claim.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I built a gold-standard labeled dataset by hand and paired it with historical claims outcomes, then trained YOLO-based object-segmentation models to localize and grade damage from photos. OpenCV handled preprocessing, and the model output fed a valuation step weighing repair cost against the vehicle's worth.

          

          

            
What I built

            
An image-to-decision pipeline that segments damage, flags likely write-offs, and estimates salvage value.

            
    
              
  • Hand-built gold-standard damage dataset
    • 
              
  • YOLO segmentation locating and grading damage
    • 
              
  • Valuation pairing damage with historical claims
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
Damage assessment moved from manual inspection to an automated, consistent estimate of write-off status and salvage value, giving adjusters a repeatable second opinion grounded in both the images and years of historical claim outcomes.

Healthcare · Rare Disease

            
Detecting rare disease in health data

            
    
              
  • Python
  • XGBoost
  • imbalanced-learn
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
Rare diseases are, by definition, almost absent from the data — a few positive cases hidden among hundreds of thousands of members. That extreme imbalance defeats models optimized for accuracy, which learn to predict "healthy" every time and miss the patients who need attention most.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I treated it as a rare-event detection problem: resampling and class-weighting to counter the imbalance, features drawn from claims and clinical history, and evaluation on positive-class precision and recall rather than raw accuracy. All built on 2019's state-of-the-art gradient-boosting and sampling techniques.

          

          

            
What I built

            
A screening model that flags members whose patterns match a rare-disease signature for clinical follow-up.

            
    
              
  • Imbalance handling via resampling and class weights
    • 
              
  • Claims and clinical-history feature engineering
    • 
              
  • Evaluation focused on rare positive-class recall
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
The model surfaced a shortlist of at-risk members from a population where the condition was vanishingly rare, turning an impossible manual search into a ranked list clinicians could actually review — catching candidates that accuracy-driven models silently ignore.

Healthcare · Fraud

            
Fraud detection for health claims

            
    
              
  • Python
  • XGBoost
  • NetworkX
    • 
            

          

          

            
The problem

            
Health-insurance fraud drains money quietly — inflated claims, phantom services, collusion between providers — and it hides in a tiny fraction of an enormous claim stream. Manual investigation can only sample; most of it goes unexamined.

          

          

            
Approach

            
I combined supervised gradient-boosted models on labeled fraud with anomaly and network analysis to catch patterns rules miss — linking providers, members and claims to expose suspicious clusters. Built with 2019's state-of-the-art techniques and tuned to rank cases by investigative value.

          

          

            
What I built

            
A claims-fraud scoring system that ranks the highest-risk claims and provider networks for investigators.

            
    
              
  • Supervised models scoring individual claims
    • 
              
  • Network analysis exposing collusion clusters
    • 
              
  • Ranked case queue prioritizing investigator time
    • 
            

          

          

            
Outcome

            
Investigators got a prioritized queue focused on the claims and provider rings most likely to be fraudulent, concentrating limited capacity where recoveries were largest instead of sampling blindly across millions of claims.

Method & honest caveats

            
TrendForce publishes quarterly DRAM contract-price change guidance (QoQ % ranges), not a public index, and no DRAM-specific series exists in FRED. The headline index is our own construction — the QoQ midpoints chained from a 2022-Q4 base — clearly labelled as illustrative, not an official price series; several 2025–26 quarters were revised sharply upward mid-quarter and we use the later figures. The FRED comparison (computer CPI, computer-storage PPI, retrieved 2026-07-12) is deliberately imperfect: neither is DRAM-specific, which is precisely why the cited TrendForce data is needed. Method & honest caveats

            
Data: the complete game collections of Morphy, Fischer, Kasparov and Carlsen from PGN Mentor, each capped at its first 600 games chronologically (Morphy has 211). Every game is re-played move-by-move with python-chess, so square-visits and captures are exact. Advantage uses raw material (1/3/3/5/9, king excluded) as a proxy for who is better — it ignores position, so the extreme buckets are noisy. Morphy's ~80% win rate reflects weak 1850s opposition, not superiority over the moderns: the scoreboard shows volume and win rate, not playing strength across eras.
